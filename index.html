About Me

I grew up in Richmond, Virginia where I first started programming as a sixth grader. Starting with building websites for my pet dogs and cats, I continued my studies of computer science, learning Java, C, and various web frameworks, and building a chess AI, a text editor, and a git-like version control system in high school. High school is also when I realized that I could use my programming skills to serve my community, where I used my technical skills to help various non-profits in Virginia. Since coming to Berkeley as an undergraduate, I've been involved with research, teaching, and community service. In the last year, I've taught myself Python, MPI, Ruby on Rails, and Erlang, and have taken a special interest in distributed computer systems, natural language processing, and educational outreach in STEM fields.

Research

UC Berkeley Department of Nuclear Engineering

Radiation Detection • February 2016 - Present

As a member of the DoseNet on UC Berkeley's and LBNL's RadWatch project, I'm working on both the hardware and software side of Raspberry Pi based dosimeters. These devices are installed in Bay Area high schools, where they help students learn about radiation and detection. (They also give us valuable data about Bay Area background radiation levels)

UC Berkeley Computer Sciences Division

Computational Game Theory • January 2016 - Present

Member of the Gamescrafters Computational Game Theory research group. Using Python with NumPy and MPI, we're building a distributed, generalized solver for two-player abstract strategy games to be deployed to Berkeley's Savio supercomputing cluster. Our current goals are to be the first researchers to strongly solve Reversi, and to make our engine open and easily usable by the public. See my contributions here.
